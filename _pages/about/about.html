About
Me

Hey! I’m Mikayla, and I’m a biologist driven by curiosity about how animals adapt to a changing world. I grew up on Cape Cod, where fishing and boating first sparked my fascination with nature—long before I ever called it science.

My Path Into Science

I began my academic journey at Cape Cod Community College, where I participated in an internship program at the Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution.

After earning my associate degree, I transferred to the University of Massachusetts Amherst and joined the MEC Lab as an undergraduate researcher in 2022. After completing my bachelor’s degree in Biology, I continued in the lab as manager for Dr. Lisa Komoroske, leading projects and supporting a diverse range of ecological and genomic research.

I’m now pursuing my Ph.D. in Organismal and Evolutionary Biology at UMass Amherst with Dr. Komoroske, where my research continues to explore how marine organisms respond and adapt to environmental change.

My Research

My research explores how marine organisms respond and adapt to a changing ocean. I focus on seascape genomics and environmental stressors, using genomic tools to uncover how temperature, oxygen, and other selective pressures shape evolutionary trajectories across complex coastal environments. Ultimately, my goal is to connect molecular patterns with ecological reality, informing conservation and management strategies as our oceans continue to change.

Access, Equity, and Open Science

As a first-generation college graduate and community college alumna, I’m deeply committed to access, equity, and inclusion in STEM. Science is stronger when more voices are included. Different perspectives enable teams to solve complex problems in ways individuals cannot achieve alone.

Those values guide both my research practices and how I support others who are just getting started. That’s why I maintain an open-access lab notebook, sharing methods, results, and lessons learned in real time. Transparency in research is more than efficiency; it’s fairness. It lowers barriers to entry, allows others to follow your process, and helps science feel like something we all build together, not something only a few deserve.

Outside the Lab

When I’m not knee-deep in data or saltwater, I’m usually experimenting in my other lab — the kitchen. Baking really is just lab work that smells better: refining protocols (recipes), running butter-to-flour ratio trials, and submitting results for peer review (a.k.a feeding friends). I even keep a Pastry Lab Notebook to record experiments, notes, and the occasional breakthrough. Outside the kitchen, I love exploring with my husband and our two dogs, Koji and Remus, who remind me daily to stay present, curious, and enjoy the process.
